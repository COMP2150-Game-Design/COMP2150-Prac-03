# Week 03 - Toys & Discovery
Today, you will be getting familiar with the Level Design Task by building a small "testing grounds" level. This level will allow you to investigate the possibility space of the core toy and how it relates to the assignment brief. This prototyping can form part of your deliverables for this assignment, or for your Design & UX Journal.

## Tools used
Today's task uses (but is not limited to):
* GitHub Desktop (or your GitHub client of choice)
* Unreal Engine

## Task 1 - Assignment Spec (5 min)
To start today, head over to iLearn and take a look at both the assignment spec for the Level Design task, and the repository on iLearn, specifically the design document template. Call over your instructor if you are struggling to find any of this.

With the person next to you, go over the assignment spec and think about any questions you might have. Is anything unclear, or is there any terminology that you don't understand? Write down these questions to ask on iLearn or during the Week 4 lecture.

## Task 2 - Design Brief (15 min)
As a class, you will now take a look at the "design brief" section of the document, which discusses the overall feel of the game, as well as prescribing particular mechanics and experiences to each section.

First, let's open up our project. Boot up the Unreal Project contained within the Assignment Repo. Note that <b>This is your assignment</b>, so you want to be working in here and pushing regularly. <b>We will not mark any Unreal projects outside of this one under any circumstance.</b>

Inside the Unreal project, you'll find a map called "LvL_TestingGrounds". Open this map. You'll see that the player character is already in there, and some geometry. This is where you are building today, and it's important you save and push this to GitHub regularly to show your work.

Your instructor will split you into pairs and assign you one section from the brief. With your partner, start brainstorming the kinds of gameplay mechanics and geography that relate to achieving the section’s requirements. Remember that brief's only real hard limits are on when the player unlocks a new ability.

You and your partner should be discussing and experimenting in Unreal Engine and creating notes/sketches. This work can feed directly into your assignment, so make sure you are capturing your own work so we know what you've made.

Your instructor will direct you on how to share your findings with the class. You want to think about:
* What are the experience goals this section calls for?
* What mechanics are you using here? What kind of geometry are you building?
* How do the mechanics and geometry contribute to these experience goals?

## Task 3 - Focusing and complicating the core (20 min)
You've now got a good handle on building small sequences within Unreal, and on understanding some of the mechanics that make for an engaging experience serving one of the goals above. With reference to the lecture, consider what the core "toy" at play is here (you've already done a bit of this in Week 1, but should have a better grip on the theory now).

What are the core mechanics? What are secondary? Once you've identified the key verbs of playing around in Cryoshock related to your section, revisit the sequences you've created. Can you structure them in a way that first accommodates simple uses of these verbs, and then complicates them? Consider which part of the play space of the Cryoshock toy you want your players to be in (if you can't remember what this means, check the lecture!).

Create a few vague structures here that don't present a linear level, but more like a tailored gymnasium. Think of this a bit like a kid's playground, where related encounters are positioned near each other to facilitate exploration.

## Task 4 - Play each other's gyms! (20 min)
Start swapping around with other students and playing their gyms. When someone is playing yours, avoid directing them. Take note of where they go and what they do. Do they seem to be engaging with the verbs you expected? Are they getting frustrated? Bored? Are they in the play space you want them to be?

After each play through, have a conversation with them, drawing on your experience from last week's process. Then, continue until you've had at least three play testers.

## Task 5 - Iterate, iterate, iterate! (Until end)
With the new knowledge from your play testers in mind, keep iterating on what you've created. Try to grab some more play testers throughout, including the instructor! A few things to consider if you get stuck:

### Parameters
Don't forget you can modify parameters on a lot of the entities in the game. If things are feeling stale or boring, how might you change one instance of a gun turret, moving platform, etc.?

### Working with constraints
There are always constraints when doing game design. Are you leaning into the constraints of the Cryoshock framework (and assignment framework), or working against them? Generally we find leaning into and finding creative solutions results in a better end experience.

### Different sections
Ultimately, you will need to build a game with all three sections. Today is a good time to experiment. If you find yourself stuck, take a look at another section, and repeat the steps above.

### Reflect
Consider what you've learnt about the game you are making a level for today. The key takeaway is an understanding of how to achieve the experiences in the design brief, and how you want to harness this in your level. We will focus on turning what you've created today into interesting encounters next week as we dive into level design theory and practice.
